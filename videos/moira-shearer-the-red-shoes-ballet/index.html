This 17-minute sequence changed the way filmmakers thought about music. Before The Red Shoes, dance scenes in movies usually tried to replicate the experience of sitting in a theater audience. Directors Powell and Pressburger broke that rule completely. They used "cinematic" magic—jump cuts, superimposition, and variable frame speeds—to take the audience inside the story of the ballet itself. The result is a surrealist nightmare that feels more like a Kate Bush or Lady Gaga video than a 1940s film. It explores the dancer’s obsession and fear, turning the "Red Shoes" fairy tale into a visual representation of sacrificing your life for art. Kate Bush was so inspired by this film that she named her 1993 album The Red Shoes after it. The executives at the studio originally hated the film and tried to bury its release, thinking the ballet sequence was too artsy and weird for general audiences. Moira Shearer originally turned down the role because she was afraid leaving the actual ballet world to make a movie would ruin her career.
